HYPERTENSION

What is Hypertension?

Hypertension is defined as systolic blood pressure (SBP) of ≥140 mmHg or/and diastolic blood pressure (DBP) of ≥ 90 mmHg or greater, on more than one occasion. Hypertension is also known as a disorder in which blood pressure remains abnormally high.

CLASSIFICATION/TYPES:

  1. Essential (primary) hypertension: of unknown cause
  2. Secondary hypertension: due to specific causes as medications or other medical problems e.g hypertension in pregnancy, liver or kidney disease.

Risk factors

  • Family history of cardiovascular disease
  • High sodium, cholesterol and saturated fat intake.
  • Increased alcohol intake.
  • Smoking.
  • Psychological and environmental stressors.
  • Old age.
  • Sedentary lifestyle.

CAUSES:

  • Stress (emotional & physical stress) filled lifestyle.
  • Unhealthy food habits -excess fat and salt intake
  • Excessive consumption of alcohol and caffeine
  • Smoking
  • Pre-Eclampsia
  • Metabolic disorder.
  • Kidney diseases.
  • Liver diseases.
  • Obesity
  • Insufficient rest and sleep
  • Certain medications e.g birth control pills

SIGNS AND SYMPTOMS

It can be asymptomatic for several years and when symptoms appear, they usually indicate vascular damage with specific manifestations related to the organ systems served by the involved vessels.

  • Dizziness
  • Blurred Vision
  • Nausea
  • Headache
  • Drowsiness
  • Nosebleeds
  • Heart Pain
  • Shortness of Breath
  • Palpitations
  • Frequent Urination etc

Prevention and Management

While there is no cure for hypertension currently, it can be prevented and successfully managed with the following steps.

  1. Restriction of sodium and fat diet.
  2. Follow an exercise program.
  3. Yoga, meditation, and relaxation.
  4. The practising of the DASH diet (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension) = more fruits, vegetables, low-fat dairy foods, whole grain, fish, poultry, nuts, foods rich in magnesium, potassium & calcium.
  5. 5. Reduce sedentary lifestyle
  6. Take anti-hypertensive drug as prescribed by your physician.
  7. Control your weight.
  8. Have regular medical check-up.
  9. Control, limit and eliminate smoking and alcohol consumption.
  10. Self-monitoring of blood pressure.

A burn is tissue damage that results from scalding, overexposure to the sun or other radiation, contact with flames, chemicals or electricity, or smoke inhalation. Grabbing a hot pot or splashing boiling water on your skin are just two common causes of burns around the house. When you get a burn, first check to see which type it is. Some are more serious than others.

  • First-degree burns are painful but minor. They turn red and may swell.
  • Second-degree burns form blisters. The skin may be very red and painful.

Serious burns need to be treated by a doctor or in a hospital. Call for medical help if:

  • You have a third-degree burn.
  • The burn is larger than 2 to 3 inches.
  • The burn is on your face, hands, feet, or over a joint like your shoulder or knee.
  • The burn goes all the way around a hand, arm, foot, or leg.
  • The pain gets worse instead of better.
  • The burn was caused by electricity or a chemical.
  • You see fluid or pus oozing from the burn.
